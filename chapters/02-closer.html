Getting Closer

When patterns became rituals

I started inviting him to study together at the library. He never refused. Not once.

I told him he didn't have to come out of politeness, that I'd understand if he was busy or just didn't feel like it. His response stayed with me:

"Being polite doesn't mean I do things I don't want to."

It was such a simple sentence. But it meant he chose to be there. That every time he showed up, it was because he wanted to, not because he felt obligated.

He walked 40 minutes from his apartment to meet me. And sometimes, when I asked what he was doing, he'd just be on Discord or researching strategies for his puzzle card game. Not even studying. Just... existing in the same space as me.

The card game explanation:

One day I asked about the game he played. He lit up immediately ‚Äî spent what felt like an hour explaining the mechanics, showing me his entire deck of cards, teaching me strategies even though I barely understood what was happening.

"If you want to learn how to play, you can ask me," he said.

I never did learn. But I loved watching him talk about it.

There was something about the way he explained things ‚Äî patient, thorough, never making me feel stupid for not understanding. Like teaching me mattered more than showing off what he knew.

He seemed nervous a lot. Especially in those first 15 minutes whenever we met. I could hear his breathing, see him scrolling aimlessly on his laptop, or suddenly lying face-down on the desk before sitting up again moments later.

The first time I invited him to the library, he went to the bathroom four times in two hours.

I didn't understand it then. Now I think I do.

Before my calculus final, I asked him how he practiced for math. He said he'd read theory, do practice problems, and then offered: "If you want to go over some practice problems together, let me know."

I said I could practice on my own. He insisted that studying with someone else was more efficient. I regretted declining almost immediately.

That night, I texted him. Asked if we could work through problems together. We booked a discussion room ‚Äî just the two of us, door closed.

The math session:

I would calculate everything in my head out of habit. He kept laughing and saying "impressive" every single time.

It wasn't impressive. It was just how I learned to do math. But the way he said it made me feel like maybe it was special.

Like I was special.

His texts were strange. Academic questions? Instant replies. But casual conversation? The response time stretched longer and longer as we got to know each other better.

Once, after a study session, he asked if I wanted to meet again. Said he'd text me the location. He never did. I had to message him first, tell him where I was, and only then did he show up.

It confused me. Still does, a little.

But when we were together, something shifted. He'd ask me how long I planned to study, tell me not to stay too late, suggest I "do something fun" instead.

Like he cared. Not in a way that demanded anything from me, but in a way that felt... gentle. Protective, almost.

"I think people who know how to cook are smart."

I said this to him once, after he explained his meal prep routine again. He laughed and said it really wasn't that hard.

But I wasn't talking about the cooking. I was talking about the kind of person who plans ahead, who takes care of themselves, who doesn't wait for someone else to fix things.

I was talking about him.

But I was starting to hope we would.

And that hope felt both terrifying and inevitable.
